The Gig Economy

Key Points of the Gig Economy

  • - Freelancers undertake specific jobs in return for an agreed-upon payment.
  • - Well-received amongst younger generations who are more accustomed to the digital world.
  • - More flexible working hours and able to work anytime, anywhere in the world.
  • - Requires a certain amount of skills and experiences to gain trust from prospect clients which can be difficult at first for beginners and inexperienced workers.
  • - No benefits such as pension funds, health insurance coverage, etc. as opposed to working in a conventional company.
  • - Experienced freelancers have more freedom in negotiating the terms to match the skills and services they may provide with a reasonable price.
  • - Online part-timers are also a subset of the gig economy as they work both in the more traditional working environment along with placing themselves in the digital world while utilizing their existing skill sets. They usually prefer to have the benefits of having a stable job and the ability to gain extra income whenever necessary.
  • - While some may think that small business owners do not qualify as freelancers, they also contribute to the local economy in a way that local services/products are marketed as equally if not better quality than imported products.

Employment in Malaysia

The minimum monthly wage in Malaysia is about RM1,100 and the unemployment rate is at 3%. According to data provided by the Ministry of Human Resources Malaysia, approximately 15,000,000 workers are employed which is almost half of the population while about 500,000 people are still unemployed or do not have any steady jobs. At least 400,000 blue-collared workers work in Singapore and thousands more work as professionals abroad in countries like Australia, UK, Japan and Europe.
